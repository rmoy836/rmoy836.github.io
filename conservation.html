Why Does Conservation Matter?

Conservation means protecting animals and their habitats so that they dont disappear from the Earth forever. This is really important because once a species goes extinct you can never bring it back.

Seals need conservation because human activities like hunting, fishing, and pollution have reduced the populations of many species. Some seals have been hunted almost to extinction and others are threatened by climate change and ocean pollution.

The good news is that when people work together to protect seals it actually works! The Northern Elephant Seal was almost completely wiped out but because of protection it has recovered to over 200,000 animals today.

Major Threats to Seals

There are several major things that threaten seal populations around the world. Here is a list of the most serious threats:

Climate Change

Melting sea ice removes the breeding and resting habitat that Arctic and Antarctic seals depend on. Baby seals can be separated from their mothers before they are old enough to survive on their own.

Fishing Nets

Seals get tangled up in fishing nets and lines by accident. This is called bycatch. Tens of thousands of seals die this way every year around the world.

Ocean Pollution

Plastic trash, oil spills, and chemical pollution get into the ocean and harm seals. These chemicals build up in their blubber and can cause health problems and make it hard for them to have babies.

Important Note: The Caribbean Monk Seal went extinct in 2008. This is the first seal species ever wiped out by humans.

Mediterranean Monk Seal

This is the most endangered seal in the entire world. There are fewer than 800 of them left. They used to live all over the Mediterranean Sea but hunting and human disturbance has pushed them to tiny isolated pockets along the coasts of Greece, Turkey, and Mauritania. They now mostly hide in sea caves because they are afraid of humans.


Hawaiian Monk Seal

The Hawaiian Monk Seal is one of only two monk seal species still alive today. There are fewer than 1,500 of them left which makes them critically endangered. They are the only seal species native to Hawaii. Scientists from NOAA actually track almost every individual seal to try and help them survive. Major threats include getting tangled in fishing gear, not enough food in the ocean, and shark attacks on baby seals.



Steller Sea Lion (Western Population)

The western population of Steller Sea Lions which lives in Alaska declined by more than 80% between the 1970s and 1990s. Scientists are still not totally sure why this happened but it is probably because of overfishing reducing their food supply and also possibly climate change affecting what fish are available.



Conservation Success Stories!

Even though the situation is serious for some seals there are also really amazing stories of seals coming back from the brink of extinction. These examples show that conservation efforts really do work!

Northern Elephant Seal

Was hunted down to only 20-100 animals by 1890. Now there are over 200,000! This is because of legal protection laws. It is one of the greatest wildlife recoveries ever!

Antarctic Fur Seal

Was hunted almost to extinction for its fur. Now protected and the population at South Georgia island has recovered to over 4 million seals. Amazing!

Guadalupe Fur Seal

Everyone thought this seal was extinct. Then in 1954 scientists found 14 of them! With protection from both the US and Mexico the population has grown to over 20,000 seals today.

How You Can Help Seals!

Things YOU Can Do

  1. Reduce plastic use
    • Use reusable bags and water bottles
    • Participate in beach cleanups in your area
    • Never litter especially near the ocean

  2. Buy sustainable seafood
    • Look for the MSC (Marine Stewardship Council) label at the store
    • Sustainable fishing means less accidental harm to seals

  3. Support conservation organizations
    • The Marine Mammal Center helps injured seals
    • NOAA works to protect Hawaiian Monk Seals
    • WWF protects many seal species worldwide

  4. Spread awareness
    • Tell your friends and family about endangered seals
    • Share information on social media
    • Make a website about seals like this one!
